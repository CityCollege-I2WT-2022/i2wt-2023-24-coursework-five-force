The attack on Pearl Harbor served as a catalyst for a rapid transformation of the American economy and society. The nation mobilized its resources for war, transitioning from a peacetime economy to a wartime footing. The war effort stimulated industrial production, leading to a surge in job opportunities and economic growth. The selective service system implemented the first peacetime draft in U.S. history, and millions of Americans willingly enlisted to serve in the armed forces. Propaganda played a crucial role in galvanizing public support for the war. Government-sponsored campaigns, posters, and films emphasized the moral imperative of defeating totalitarian regimes and preserving democratic values. Rosie the Riveter became an iconic symbol, representing the millions of women who entered the workforce to support the war effort. The media, through newsreels and radio broadcasts, played a vital role in shaping public opinion and maintaining morale on the home front.
